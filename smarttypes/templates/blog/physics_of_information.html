First the backstory, my fondness for online social networks

I'll start @ an unlikely starting point.

The book What Hath God Wrought: The Transformation of America, 1815 - 1848 describes a shift in the physics of information. The physics of information is a cool topic on it's own. I'll suppress the urge right now.

What Hath God Wrought highlights a story published by a British paper shortly after the telegraph was up and running. The story describes a man killing his wife, and boarding a train before the information of his crime spread. A description of the man was telegraphed ahead of the train, and the man was caught.

The fact that authorities were able to catch the man isn't very amazing to us. But, for people in the mid-1800s, information traveling a million times faster than a train or horse or boat was extraordinary. Before the mid-1800s, human digestible information, literally, could not travel faster than a train.

The book highlights a few other stories like this. Andrew Jackson's famous Battle of New Orleans took place after the British surrendered (it takes a long time to send a message by boat).

I love the idea of overcoming old physical limitations.

I also love this: Twitter mood predicts the stock market.

You may have seen this. If not, you may want to read that again. Twitter mood predicts the stock market. That's no small feat. The financial world spends a lot of money on computer algorithms to predict the future. A lot of money is really an understatement. Why would twitter be so good at predicting the market?

So, with a growing appreciation for online social networks, i decided to focus the little extra time i had on building a program to analyse twitter, my favorite social network.

Biological networks are messy and complicated

For some reason or another i decided my first project was going to be clustering my twitter network. Putting people into similar groups using only their network information (who they follow, and who follows them). This is called graph clustering or community detection.

Being a python programmer (i love python's emphasis on simplicity, and its growing adoption in scientific circles) i starting looking for python libraries related to graph clustering. I found networkx.

I also starting looking for, and following people w/ similar interests on twitter.

I found a lot of info on networks and graphs. The networkx community was a great launchpad. See the networkx overview and the networkx GSoC2011 announcement. Specifically, i read Community detection in graphs and Newman's Networks: An Introduction. I learned about network adjacency matrices, and established graph clustering techniques.

Around this time i also met Valdis Krebs on twitter.

Valdis stressed the messy/nonlinear/complex nature of social networks.

This was a key moment for me. I can't always do it, but sometimes i imagine us all as a crazy, interconnected, super-organism. A lot of other people have had this thought, you've probably heard the term 'the human super-organism'. From this perspective human social networks look like neural networks, or genetic regulatory networks, or the weather, ect... All these systems are messy, biological, and complex. We need machines to help make sense of these things.

Try to imagine a model w/ a hundred or a thousand or a million dimensions. Now you understand why we need computers.
